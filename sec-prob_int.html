    Section 5.1 Definition

    If a random experiment has total of \((m+n)\) mutually exclusive, mutually exhaustive, and equally likely outcomes, out of which there are \(m\) favorable events \(E\text{,}\) then the probability of occurrence of event \(E\) is denoted by \(P(E).\)
    \begin{equation} P(E) = \frac{\text { favorable outcomes}}{ \text{ total outcomes}}\tag{5.1.1} \end{equation}
    In sample space,
    \begin{equation*} S = \{x_1, x_2, x_3, \ldots, x_n \} \end{equation*}
    each sample points are equally likely events, then we can find the probability of occurrence using (5.1.1). However, if they are not equally likely then (5.1.1) can not be used directly.
    Note: If all outcomes of sample space are not equally likely, then
    \begin{equation*} P(E) = \text { sum of probability of all the favorable outcomes of E.} \end{equation*}
    here, in sample space,
    \begin{equation*} S = \{x_1, x_2, x_3, \ldots, x_n \} \end{equation*}
    \(x_1, x_2, \ldots \) are not equally likely outcomes, then (5.1.1) cannot be applied.

    Example 5.1.1.

    A coin is tossed, if it shows head then toss it again, otherwise throw a dice. Here,
    1. \(E_1\) = getting two heads.
    2. \(E_2\) = getting an odd number.
    3. \(E_3\) = getting tail in outcome.
    Solution.
    The sample space,
    \begin{equation*} S = \{(H,H), (H,T), (T,1), (T,2), (T,3), (T,4), (T,5), (T,6)\} \end{equation*}
    has total outcomes = 8, but each outcome has different probability, i.e., they are not equally likely.
    1. \begin{equation*} E_1 = \{(H,H)\} \end{equation*}
    2. \begin{equation*} E_2 = \{(T,1), (T,3), (T,5)\} \end{equation*}
    3. \begin{equation*} E_3 = \{(H,T), (T,1), (T,2), (T,3), (T,4), (T,5), (T,6)\} \end{equation*}
    The probability of event \(E_1\) is given by
    1. \begin{equation*} P(E_1) = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4}. \end{equation*}
      The probability of \(1/2\) for getting \(H\) and \(1/2\) for getting \(T\text{.}\)
    2. \begin{equation*} P(E_2) = \frac{1}{12} + \frac{1}{12} + \frac{1}{12} = \frac{1}{4}. \end{equation*}
      The probability of \(1/2\) for getting \(T\) in a coin and \(1/6\) for getting \(1\) or \(3\text{,}\) etc in a dice.
    3. \begin{equation*} P(E_3) = \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{12} + \frac{1}{12}+ \frac{1}{12}+ \frac{1}{12}+ \frac{1}{12}+ \frac{1}{12} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \therefore P(E_3) = \frac{1}{4} + \frac{6}{12} = \frac{3}{4}. \end{equation*}
      The probability of \(1/4\) for getting \((H,T)\) and \(1/12\) for getting \((T,1), (T,2), \ldots \text{.}\)
    Important Note:
    1. Sum of probabilities of each sample point = 1.
      or, probability of sample space = 1.
      If sample space,
      \begin{equation*} S = \{ x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n \} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P{(x_i)}=1. \end{equation*}
    2. If
      \begin{equation*} P(E) = 0, \end{equation*}
      then \(E\) is impossible event.
      If
      \begin{equation*} P(E) = 1, \end{equation*}
      then \(E\) is sure or certain event.
      Hence,
      \begin{equation*} 0 \leq P(E) \leq 1. \end{equation*}
    3. Probability of non-occurrence of event,
      \begin{equation*} E = P(\bar{E}) = 1-P(E). \end{equation*}

    Subsection 5.1.1 Useful Set-Formulae for Probability

    \begin{align} P(A') \amp = P(\bar{A}) = 1-P(A) \tag{5.1.2}\\ P(A\cap B)' \amp = 1-P(A \cap B) = P(A' \cup B') \tag{5.1.3}\\ P(A \text{ or }B ) \amp = P(A\cup B) = P(A) + P(B) -P(A\cap B) \tag{5.1.4}\\ P(A \text{ but not } B) \amp = P(A\cap B') = P(A) -P(A\cap B) \tag{5.1.5}\\ P(\text{ not } A \text{ not } B) \amp = P(A' \cap B') = 1-P(A\cup B) \tag{5.1.6}\\ P(\text{neither } A \text{ nor } B) \amp = P(A' \cup B') = P(A \cap B)' = 1-P(A \cap B) \tag{5.1.7}\\ (A\cup B)' \amp = A' \cap B' \qquad \text{ De Morgan's I law} \tag{5.1.8}\\ (A\cap B)' \amp = A' \cup B' \qquad \text{ De Morgan's II law} \tag{5.1.9} \end{align}

    Subsection 5.1.2 Conditional probability:

    It is the possibility of an event that based on the existence of a previous event or outcome. In other words, The probability of occurrence of any event A when another event B in relation to A has already occurred is known as conditional probability. It is depicted by \(P(A|B)\text{,}\) here "|" read as given. So \(P(A|B)\) reads as probability of A given B.
    Imagine a student is absent from school twice a week, excluding Sunday. If it is known that he will be absent from school on Tuesday, then what are the chances that he will also be absent on Friday in the same week? It is observed that in problems where the occurrence of one event affects the happening of the following event, these cases of probability are known as conditional probability.
    \begin{align} P(A|B) \amp = \frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)}; \quad P(B) \neq 0. \quad \text{ conditional Prob.}\tag{5.1.10}\\ or, \quad P(A|B) \amp = \frac{N(A\cap B)}{N(B)}; \quad N(B) \neq 0. \quad \text{ conditional Prob.} \tag{5.1.11} \end{align}
    here \(N(A\cap B)\) is the number of elements common to both A and B. \(N(B)\) is the number of elements in B, and it cannot be equal to zero.
    Bayes Theorem: Bayes’ theorem defines the probability of occurrence of an event associated with any condition. This is known as the formula for the likelihood of “causes”.
    \begin{equation} or, \quad P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A)P(A)}{P(B)}\tag{5.1.12} \end{equation}

    Subsection 5.1.3 Rules of Probability

    1. Additional Rule: It is used to calculate the probability that either (or both) of two events will happen. Notation: \(P(A \cup B), P(A + B), P(A \text{ or } B), P( \text{ either} A \text { or} B), P(\text{ at least } 1).\)
      1. For mutually exclusive (disjoint) events (A) and (B) [events that cannot happen at the same time], the probability of (A) or (B) occuring is
        \begin{equation} P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B).\tag{5.1.13} \end{equation}
        For example: tossing a coin once .
        The probability of getting a Head or a Tail on tossing a coin.
        \begin{equation*} P(H \cup T) = P(H) + P(T) = \frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{2} = 1. \end{equation*}
      2. For non-mutually exclusive events (A) and (B) [events that can happen at the same time], the probability of (A) or (B) occuring is
        \begin{equation} P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)-P(A\cap B).\tag{5.1.14} \end{equation}
        For example: drawing a king of heart from a deck of card.
        The probability of getting a King of Heart on drawing a card from a deck of cards.
        \begin{equation*} P(K \cup H) = P(K) + P(H) -P(K\cap H) = \frac{4}{52}+\frac{13}{52}-\frac{1}{52} = \frac{16}{52}. \end{equation*}
    2. Multiplication Rule: It allows us to calculate the joint probability of multiple events occurring together using known probabilities of those events individually.
      1. For Independent events (A) and (B) [events where occurance of one does not affect the other], the probability of (A) and (B) occuring is
        \begin{equation} P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B).\tag{5.1.15} \end{equation}
        For example: tossing a coin twice. Tossing a coin first time does not affect what it comes in the second time. In independent events, total number of outcomes do not reduce.
        The probability of getting a 1 and then a 3 on rolling a fair dice.
        \begin{equation*} P(1 \cap 3) = P(1) \times P(3) = \frac{1}{6}\times \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{36}. \end{equation*}
      2. For dependent events (A) and (B) [events where occurance of one does affect the other], the probability of (A) and (B) occuring is
        \begin{equation} P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B|A).\tag{5.1.16} \end{equation}
        Where \(P(B|A)\) is a conditional probability of (B) given (A). For example: Drawing I card affect the drawing of the II card without replacement. In dependent events, total number of outcomes reduce.
        The probability of getting a King and then a Queen on drawing cards from a deck without replacement.
        \begin{equation*} P(K \cap Q) = P(K) \times P(Q/A) = \frac{1}{52}\times \frac{1}{51} = \frac{1}{2652}. \end{equation*}
