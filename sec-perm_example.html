    Section 4.3 Example A

    Example 4.3.9.

    Evaluate: \(^{12}P_4.\)
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} ^{12}P_4 \amp = \frac{12!}{(12-4)!} \\ \amp = \frac{12\times 11 \times 10 \times 9 \times \cancel{8!}}{\cancel{8!}} \\ \amp = 12\times 11 \times 10 \times 9 = 11880. \end{align*}

    Example 4.3.10.

    Find the value of \(n\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{ ^nP_4}{ ^{(n-1)}P_4}= \frac{5}{3}, \quad n \gt 4. \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} \frac{\frac{ n!}{ (n-4)!}}{\frac{ (n-1)!}{ (n-1-4)!}} \amp = \frac{5}{3} \\ \text { or,} \quad \frac{\frac{ n(n-1)(n-2)(n-3)\cancel{(n-4)!}}{ \cancel{(n-4)!}}}{\frac{ (n-1)(n-2)(n-3)(n-4)\cancel{(n-5)!}}{ \cancel{(n-5)!}}} \amp = \frac{5}{3} \\ \text { or,} \quad \frac{ n \cancel{(n-1)(n-2)(n-3)}}{ \cancel{(n-1)(n-2)(n-3)}(n-4)} \amp = \frac{5}{3} \\ \text { or,} \quad \frac{n}{n-4}\amp =\frac{5}{3}\\ \text { or,} \quad 3n \amp =5n -20\\ \therefore \quad n \amp =10. \end{align*}

    Example 4.3.11.

    If \(5 \times ^4P_r = 6 \times ^5P_{(r-1)}, \) find r.
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} 5 \times \frac{4!}{(4-r)!} \amp = 6 \times \frac{5!}{(5-(r-1))!}\\ or, \quad \frac{\cancel{5\times 4!}}{(4-r)!} \amp = \frac{ 6 \times \cancel{5 \times 4!}}{(6-r)!}\\ or, \quad \frac{1}{(4-r)!} \amp = \frac{6}{(6-r)!} \\ or, \quad \frac{1}{\cancel{(4-r)!}} \amp = \frac{6}{(6-r)(5-r)\cancel{(4-r)!}} \\ or, \quad (6-r)(5-r) \amp = 6\\ or, \quad (6-r)(5-r) \amp = 3\times 2 \end{align*}
    by equating LHS and RHS, we get -
    \begin{align*} 6-r \amp = 3 \\ and \quad 5-r \amp = 2 \\ \therefore \quad r \amp = 3. \end{align*}

    Example 4.3.12.

    If \(^{15}P_r = 2730,\) find the value of \(r.\)
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} ^{15}P_r \amp = 2730 \\ or, \quad \frac{15!}{(15-r)!} \amp = 2\times 3 \times 5 \times7 \times 13\\ or, \quad \frac{15!}{(15-r)!} \amp = 15 \times 14 \times 13 \\ or, \quad \frac{\cancel{15!}}{(15-r)!} \amp = \frac{\cancel{15!}}{12!}\\ (15-r)! \amp = 12! \\ \therefore \quad r \amp = 3. \end{align*}

    Example 4.3.13.

    If \(^{56}P_(r+6) : ^{54}P_(r+3) = (30800:1),\) find the value of \(r.\)
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} \frac{56!}{[56-(r+6)]!} \times \frac{(54-r-3)!}{54!}\amp = \frac{30800}{1}\\ or, \quad \frac{56\times 55}{(50-r)!} \times \frac{(51-r)!}{1}\amp = \frac{55\times 56 \times 10}{1}\\ or, \quad \frac{(51-r)!}{(50-r)!}\amp = 10\\ or, \quad \frac{(51-r)}{1}\amp = 10 \\ or, \quad 51-r \amp = 10 \\ \therefore \quad r \amp = 51-10 = 41. \end{align*}

    Example 4.3.14.

    Prove that \(^nP_n = ^nP_{(n-1)}\)
    Solution.
    LHS,
    \begin{equation*} ^nP_n = \frac{n!}{(n-n)!} = \frac{n!}{0!} = n! \end{equation*}
    RHS,
    \begin{equation*} ^nP_{(n-1)} = \frac{n!}{(n-n+1)!} =\frac{n!}{1!} = n! \end{equation*}
    see Section4.2

    Example 4.3.15.

    3 men have 4 coats, 6 paints, and 5 caps. In how many ways can they wear them?
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c} coats & paints & caps \\ \hline ^4P_3 & ^6P_3 & ^5P_3 \\ \end{array} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = ^4P_3\times ^6P_3 \times ^5P_3 \end{equation*}
    each of these outfits are compulsory to complete dressed up for the men, hence multiplication principle is used here to find the total ways of wearing them.
    \begin{equation*} =\frac{4!}{1!} \times \frac{6!}{3!}\times \frac{5!}{2!} = \frac{4!\times 6\times 5\times \times 4!\times 5\times 4!}{3!\times 2!} = 172800. \end{equation*}

    Example 4.3.16.

    How many different signals can be made by using any number of flags from \(4\) flags of different colors?
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c} \text{ I signal} & \text{ II signal} & \text{ III signal} & \text{ IV signal} \\ \hline ^4P_1 & ^4P_2 & ^4P_3 & ^4P_4 \\ \end{array} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = ^4P_1\times ^4P_2 \times ^4P_3 \times ^4P_4 \end{equation*}
    Every signals are complete and independent on each other, hence addition principle is used to find the total numbers of signals.
    \begin{equation*} =\frac{4!}{3!} + \frac{4!}{2!}\times \frac{4!}{1!} \times \frac{4!}{0!} = 4+12+24+24 = 64. \end{equation*}

    Example 4.3.17.

    A code is to consist of two distinct letters followed by two distinct numbers between 1 and 9. For example, CA35, DQ72, etc. are codes.
    1. How many such codes are there?
    2. How many of them end with an even integer?
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c} \text{ I alphabet} & \text{ II alphabet} & \text{ I digit} & \text{ II digit} \\ \hline 26 & 25 & 9 & 8 \\ \end{array} \end{equation*}
    1. We need distinct alphabets and digits to make codes. Hence the total codes are \(26\times 25\times 9\times 8 = 46800. \text{.}\)
      or, we have -
      \begin{equation*} = ^{26}P_2\times ^9P_2 = \frac{26!}{24!} \times \frac{9!}{7!} = 26\times 25\times 9\times 8 = 46800. \end{equation*}
    2. For even number on units place there are 4 choices \(2, 4, 6, 8\) and we need distinct numbers on each place so remaining 8-digits are only available for tens place. Hence,
      \begin{equation*} = ^{26}P_2\times 8 \times 4 = 26\times 25\times 8\times 4 = 20800. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c} \text{ I alphabet} & \text{ II alphabet} & \text{ I digit} & \text{ even no.} \\ \hline 26 & 25 & 8 & 4 \\ \end{array} \end{equation*}

    Example 4.3.18.

    Find the number of ways in which 5 boys and 3 girls can be arranged in a row so that no two girls come together.
    Solution.
    To have no two girls together, we can arrange boys first and then place each girl between them or before or after the first and last boys.
    B1 B2 B3 B4 B5 .
    \begin{equation*} ^5P_5 \times ^6P_3 = 5! \times 6 \times 5 \times 4 = 14400. \end{equation*}
    here 6 represents numbers of seats available for girls.

    \begin{equation*} ^3P_3 \times ^2P_2 \times ^3P_3 \times ^4P_4 = 3! \times 2! \times 3! \times 4! = ...... \end{equation*}

    Example 4.3.20.

    In how many ways can 7 test papers be arranged so that the best and the worst papers are never come together?
    Solution.
    P1, P2, P3, P4, P5, P6, P7
    Here, the total arrangements are \(^7P_7\) for all papers together.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c} \text{ P1, P2, P3, P4, P5 } & \text{ P6, P7 } \\ \hline \text{ 5 object} & \text{ 1 object} \\ \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Now, number of ways in which the best and the worst papers come together is given by \(^6P_6 \times ^2P_2\text{.}\) First bundle of papers \(P1, P2, P3, P4, P5\) considered as 5 objects. and the second bundle \(P6, P7 \) is considered as 1 object, as they are coming together.
    Hence, the number of ways when best and worst papers never come together is given by
    \begin{equation*} ^7P_7 - \left( ^6P_6 \times ^2P_2 \right) = 7! -(6!\times 2!) = .... \end{equation*}

    Example 4.3.30.

    In how many ways can 3 prizes be distributed among 4 boys, when
    1. No boys get more than 1 prize,
    2. A boy may get any number of prizes,
    3. No boy gets all the prizes?
    Solution.
    Here, we have prizes: \(P1, P2, P3\) and boys: \(B1, B2, B3, B4\)
    1. First prize can be received by any one of the boys, so 4 choice, and 3 choice, and so on. \(4\times 3 \times 2 \times 1 = 4!.\)
    2. \(\displaystyle 4\times 4 \times 4 \times 4 = 4^3 = 64.\)
    3. Boy gets all the prizes - all prizes go to any one of the boys. \(64-4 =60.\)
